About

I combine econometric reasoning, operations-style modeling, and practical data engineering to evaluate high-impact technology and policy questions. I am particularly interested in how compute, energy, and supply-chain constraints shape the trajectory of frontier AI.

My background includes predictive modeling and decision-support systems in automotive and mobility contexts, plus strategy consulting projects in digital commercialization and market expansion.

Research Directions

Frontier AI Compute Economics

Modeling bottlenecks in semiconductors, packaging, data-center capacity, and power systems to understand cost trajectories and strategic behavior.

Market Design and Governance

Applying game-theoretic and mechanism-design intuition to AI policy questions, including allocation, access, and competition in constrained compute markets.

Empirical Policy Evaluation

Using quasi-experimental and predictive methods to estimate policy and operational impacts, with outputs designed for non-technical decision makers.

Current Working Paper

Chips, Power, and Policy: How Frontier AI Compute Constraints Shift from Silicon to Energy

Quantitative working paper on GPU/TPU supply, advanced packaging bottlenecks, and emerging electricity constraints in the deployment of frontier models.

The project connects semiconductor and power-system constraints to policy design for resilient and socially aligned AI development.
